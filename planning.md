# Provenance Guard Planning

## Project Overview

Provenance Guard is a backend system that analyzes submitted text and estimates whether it was likely written by a human or generated by AI. The system is not meant to punish creators. It is meant to give readers transparency and give creators a way to appeal if they believe their work was misclassified.

A submitted piece of text enters the system through POST /submit. The system validates the input, runs two detection signals, combines them into a confidence score, creates a transparency label, saves the decision to the audit log, and returns the result.

If a creator disagrees with the decision, they can submit an appeal through POST /appeal. The system records their reasoning, updates the content status to under_review, and logs the appeal for a future human reviewer.

Detection Signals
Signal 1: Groq LLM Classification

The first signal uses Groq with llama-3.3-70b-versatile to evaluate whether a piece of text appears AI-generated or human-written.

This signal captures overall writing style, tone, structure, and semantic patterns. It can notice polished, generic, overly balanced, or formulaic writing that may not be obvious through simple statistics.

Output:

{
  "llm_score": 0.0
}

The score ranges from 0.0 to 1.0.

0.0 means strongly human-written.
0.5 means uncertain.
1.0 means strongly AI-generated.

Blind spots:

The LLM can be wrong.
Formal human writing may look AI-generated.
Edited AI writing may look human.
Short text may not contain enough evidence.
Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

The second signal uses measurable writing patterns in pure Python.

Metrics:

Sentence length variance
Human writing often has more uneven sentence lengths.
AI writing can be more uniform.
Type-token ratio
This measures vocabulary diversity.
Lower diversity may suggest generic or repetitive writing.
Punctuation density
Human writing may use punctuation more irregularly.
AI writing may use cleaner, more predictable punctuation.

Output:

{
  "stylometric_score": 0.0
}

The score ranges from 0.0 to 1.0.

0.0 means the structure looks more human.
0.5 means unclear.
1.0 means the structure looks more AI-generated.

Blind spots:

Poems and creative writing may have unusual structure.
Very short text may produce unreliable metrics.
Academic human writing may appear polished and uniform.
Non-native English writing may be misread as AI-like.
Confidence Scoring

The final AI-likelihood score combines both signals.

Formula:

final_score = (llm_score * 0.60) + (stylometric_score * 0.40)

I am weighting the LLM signal slightly more because it captures meaning, tone, and overall writing style. The stylometric signal still matters because it provides an independent structural check.

The final score ranges from 0.0 to 1.0.

0.00–0.39: likely human-written
0.40–0.69: uncertain
0.70–1.00: likely AI-generated

A score of 0.60 means the system sees some AI-like signals, but not enough to make a confident attribution. This should result in an uncertain label rather than a strong accusation.

False positives are a serious concern because wrongly labeling a human creator as AI-generated could harm their credibility. Because of this, the system uses an uncertain middle range instead of forcing every result into human or AI.

Transparency Label Design
High-confidence AI label

"Likely AI-generated: Our system found strong evidence that this content may have been generated using AI. Confidence: {confidence}%. Creators may appeal this decision if they believe it is incorrect."

High-confidence human label

"Likely human-written: Our system found strong evidence that this content was written by a person. Confidence: {confidence}%."

Uncertain label

"Uncertain attribution: Our system could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or generated using AI. Confidence: {confidence}%. This label is not a final judgment."

Appeals Workflow

A creator can submit an appeal if they believe their content was misclassified.

The appeal request must include:

content_id
creator_reasoning

When an appeal is received, the system will:

Look up the original content decision.
Update the content status from classified to under_review.
Store the creator's reasoning.
Add an appeal event to the audit log.
Return a confirmation response.

A human reviewer would see:

original content ID
creator ID
original attribution result
original confidence score
individual signal scores
transparency label
creator appeal reasoning
updated status

Automated re-classification is not required for this version.

Rate Limiting Plan

The POST /submit endpoint will use Flask-Limiter.

Chosen limits:

10 submissions per minute
100 submissions per day

Reasoning:

A normal creator is unlikely to submit more than 10 pieces of writing in one minute. The per-minute limit helps prevent scripts from flooding the system. The daily limit still allows active users to test or submit many pieces in a day while preventing abuse.

Anticipated Edge Cases
Edge Case 1: Poetry or highly stylized writing

Poems may use repetition, short lines, unusual punctuation, and limited vocabulary. The stylometric signal might incorrectly score this as AI-like because the structure is unusual.

Edge Case 2: Formal academic writing

A human-written academic paragraph may be polished, balanced, and structured. Both the LLM signal and stylometric signal might score it as AI-like even if it was written by a person.

Edge Case 3: Very short submissions

A sentence or two may not provide enough evidence for either signal. In these cases, the system should lean toward uncertain instead of making a strong attribution.

Edge Case 4: Non-native English writing

A non-native English speaker may write in a more formal or patterned way. The system could incorrectly treat that as AI-like, so the appeals workflow is important.
